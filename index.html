What You Must Do

You are a monarch — the ruler of a fictional kingdom. A moral dilemma has arisen in your realm, and you must judge it.

You will write an 800-word Ethical Issues Analysis in which you:

1. Identify a moral dilemma

Choose one of the stimulus materials. What is the core ethical conflict?

2. Consult your two philosophical advisors

Each advisor represents a different ethical theory. They each analyse the dilemma using their theory's reasoning.

3. Weigh their advice

Analyse the strengths and weaknesses of each advisor's reasoning, including counterarguments.

4. Make your ruling

Decide which advisor was more convincing, make your judgement, and explain what happens next.

The Structure (800 words)

Introduction (~100 words)

What is the moral dilemma? What exactly are you judging (a situation, action, or person) and why? How will you judge it? Which two theories are your advisors?

Theory 1 (~200 words)

Outline the ethical claims your first advisor makes. Explain, step-by-step, the reasoning they use to evaluate the dilemma. Remember to explain all key concepts.

Theory 2 (~200 words)

In contrast, how does your second advisor see things differently? Explain their reasoning step-by-step. Highlight where the two theories disagree.

Strengths & Weaknesses (~200 words)

Analyse the strengths and weaknesses of each advisor's reasoning, including counterarguments. This is the hardest section — you're evaluating the theories themselves.

Your Verdict (~100 words)

Which advisor was more convincing, and why? Make your ruling. Explain what happens next in your kingdom.

A utilitarian would say that what makes people happy is good and what makes people unhappy is bad. In this case, the people who are being discriminated against are unhappy, so discrimination is wrong according to utilitarianism. This is because utilitarians care about happiness.

A utilitarian evaluates this dilemma by applying the Greatest Happiness Principle: the right action is the one that produces the greatest balance of happiness over unhappiness for all affected. CA

Which of these shows accurate and perceptive analysis of strengths and weaknesses? Click the stronger one.

The utilitarian approach has the strength of being practical — by focusing on measurable harm, it provides a clear basis for action. However, it has a significant weakness: it could justify any action as long as the outcome produces net happiness, including violating individual rights. For instance, if silencing a minority group somehow produced greater overall happiness, a strict utilitarian would have to accept it. The deontological approach avoids this problem by protecting rights absolutely, but its rigidity is also its weakness. Kant would say we must treat all people as ends in themselves — but what if two duties conflict, such as the duty to tell the truth and the duty to protect life?

The strength of utilitarianism is that it cares about people's happiness. The weakness is that sometimes it doesn't work. Deontology is good because it has clear rules, but sometimes the rules are too strict. Both theories have strengths and weaknesses.

C/D-Grade CA just labels things as "good" or "bad" without explaining why. "Sometimes it doesn't work" is not analysis. There are no counterexamples, no specific reasoning, and the final sentence is an empty summary.

Step 1 of 9

Step 1: Choose Your Stimulus

The very first thing you need to do is choose a stimulus — a short film, documentary, or text that presents a real ethical situation.

This is the foundation of your entire essay. Everything you write flows from this choice.

What to look for in a stimulus:

  • A clear moral conflict — someone faces a tough choice
  • It should make you think “Was that the right thing to do?”
  • You need to feel engaged enough to write 800 words about it

Available stimuli include:

Short films like Two and Two, Snack Attack, Alike, The Present, 2081, and documentaries like The House I Live In and Louis Theroux: Inside the Manosphere. You’ll get to watch and choose during the game.

Step 2: Identify the Moral Dilemma

Once you’ve chosen your stimulus, you need to write down the moral dilemma — the core ethical conflict at the heart of the story.

A moral dilemma is:

A situation where there is genuine disagreement about what the right thing to do is. It’s not just “something bad happened” — it’s a conflict between competing moral values.

  • Good example: “Is it morally justified for a government to enforce equality by handicapping its strongest and smartest citizens?”
  • Weak example: “Harrison Bergeron is about equality.”

Frame it as a question:

The best moral dilemmas are phrased as questions that two reasonable people could disagree on. This sets up the debate between your two theories.

Step 3: Synthesise Into a Case

Now comes the creative part. You need to synthesise that moral dilemma into a case that you, as a ruler, must judge.

You’re not just writing an essay — you’re a monarch presiding over a moral dispute in your kingdom. The stimulus becomes the case brought before your court.

Your introduction should:

  • Briefly describe the situation from the stimulus
  • State the moral dilemma clearly
  • Explain that you will consult two philosophical advisors to help you judge
  • Name your two theories

Remember: This is ~100 words. Be concise. Set the scene, state the dilemma, name your advisors.

Step 4: Outline Two Theories

This is crucial. You don’t just write about “Utilitarianism” or “Deontology” in the abstract. You must present each theory according to specific philosophers.

You CANNOT just say “Utilitarianism says...” — you must say “According to Mill...” or “As Rachels explains...”

Philosophers for each theory:

  • Utilitarianism: Mill, Singer, or Rachels
  • Deontology: Kant, or Rachels
  • Virtue Ethics: Aristotle, or Rachels
  • Ethical Egoism: Rand, or Rachels

Tip: James Rachels can be used for ANY theory — his textbook covers all four. So if you’re stuck, Rachels is always a safe bet alongside the main philosopher.

Step 5: Explain the Logic of Each Theory

Each theory section is ~200 words. You must outline the logic of the theory in a way that leads to its key concepts. This is what the assessment criteria are looking for:

Assessment Criteria

KU1 Knowledge and understanding of philosophical issues, concepts, and positions

R1 Reasoning using evidence to support philosophical claims

What this means in practice:

Don’t just name-drop concepts. Build the argument step by step:

  • Start with the theory’s core principle (e.g., “Mill argues that the right action is the one that produces the greatest happiness for the greatest number”)
  • Explain the key concept that flows from this (e.g., “This is known as the Principle of Utility”)
  • Show how one concept leads to the next (e.g., “From this principle, we can see that consequences are what matter, not intentions”)
  • Each concept should feel like a natural step in the argument, not a random fact

Think of it as building a chain: Principle → Key Concept → Next Concept → Application. Each link should connect logically.

Step 6: Apply Each Theory to the Case

After explaining each theory, you need to apply it directly to the moral dilemma from your stimulus. This is where the theory meets reality.

Assessment Criteria

CA1 Critical analysis of the strengths and weaknesses of philosophical positions

What good application looks like:

  • Name the specific characters or situations from the stimulus
  • Show what the theory would say about the specific moral choice
  • Use the key concepts you’ve already explained
  • Make it clear you’re not just summarising the film — you’re using the theory as a lens

Example:

“A utilitarian like Mill would argue that the Handicapper General’s system in 2081 fails the Principle of Utility because the total happiness in society is reduced, not increased — people are suffering under their handicaps, and the only moment of genuine joy (Harrison’s dance) is immediately destroyed.”

Step 7: Provide Objections

The Strengths & Weaknesses section (~200 words) is the hardest and most important. You need to critique each theory within the context of the case.

Assessment Criteria

CA1 Critical analysis of the strengths and weaknesses of philosophical positions

R1 Reasoning using evidence to support philosophical claims

How to write strong objections:

  • Be specific to your case — don’t just list generic weaknesses
  • Use phrases like: “See, this is the problem with Deontology — it is too rigid” or “The weakness of Utilitarianism here is that it ignores individual rights”
  • Acknowledge what each theory gets right as well as what it gets wrong
  • Show that you understand why the theory fails in this particular situation

Example:

“However, the problem with the deontological position is its rigidity. Kant would say we have an absolute duty not to use people as mere means — but in 2081, the government claims it has a duty to maintain social order. When two duties conflict, Deontology offers no clear way to decide which takes priority.”

Step 8: Your Verdict

Your conclusion (~100 words) is where you, the ruler, make your judgement. But this is not just an opinion — your position must be rationally based on what the theories have offered.

Assessment Criteria

C1 Communication of philosophical ideas with clarity

C2 Use of appropriate philosophical terminology

Your verdict must:

  • Refer back to the theories — explain which advisor was more convincing and why
  • Be grounded in reasoning — not just “I think X is right” but “Given the strengths of Theory A and the weaknesses of Theory B, I rule that...”
  • Make a clear ruling about the moral dilemma
  • Optionally: describe what happens next in your kingdom as a result of the ruling

Introduction (~100 words)

In the short film 2081, the government enforces total equality by handicapping anyone with above-average abilities. The moral dilemma at the heart of this case is: does the government have the right to limit individual excellence for the sake of social harmony? As the ruler of this court, I will consult two philosophical advisors. My first advisor, representing Utilitarianism (as outlined by Mill and Rachels), will examine whether this system maximises happiness. My second advisor, representing Deontology (as outlined by Kant and Rachels), will consider whether it respects people’s fundamental rights and dignity.

Theory 1: Utilitarianism (~200 words)

My first advisor explains that according to John Stuart Mill, the morally right action is the one that produces the greatest happiness for the greatest number. This is known as the Principle of Utility. Mill distinguishes between higher pleasures (intellectual, creative) and lower pleasures (physical, passive), arguing that higher pleasures are more valuable. As Rachels explains, utilitarianism demands that we consider the consequences of actions impartially — everyone’s happiness counts equally.

Applying this to the case of 2081, my utilitarian advisor argues that the handicapping system fails the Principle of Utility. George Bergeron suffers constant pain from his earpiece; ballerinas dance clumsily under weights; brilliant minds cannot hold a thought. The total suffering in society has increased, not decreased. Furthermore, by destroying higher pleasures (art, intellect, beauty), the government has eliminated precisely the forms of happiness Mill values most. Harrison’s brief, beautiful dance was the only moment of genuine higher pleasure in the film — and it was immediately destroyed.

Theory 2: Deontology (~200 words)

In contrast, my second advisor explains that Immanuel Kant argues morality is about duty and universal moral rules, not consequences. The Categorical Imperative states that we should only act according to rules we could will to be universal laws. Crucially, Kant insists we must always treat people as ends in themselves, never merely as means to an end. As Rachels notes, Kant believed that rational beings have inherent dignity that cannot be traded away for any benefit.

Applying this to 2081, my deontological advisor argues that the handicapping system treats citizens as mere means to the government’s goal of enforced equality. George is not respected as a rational being — his very ability to think is deliberately disrupted. The system cannot pass the Categorical Imperative: we could not rationally will a universal law that says “destroy everyone’s best qualities.” The Handicapper General uses people as tools for social policy, violating their fundamental dignity.

Strengths & Weaknesses (~200 words)

Both advisors make compelling points, but each has weaknesses in this case. The strength of the utilitarian approach is that it clearly demonstrates the measurable suffering the system causes — we can see the pain, the lost potential, the destroyed beauty. However, the problem with Utilitarianism here is that it could potentially justify the handicapping system if the majority actually were happier as a result. If most people feel more comfortable without the pressure of competing with exceptional individuals, a strict utilitarian calculation might support the system. This reveals a troubling implication: Utilitarianism might sacrifice individual rights for majority comfort.

The deontological approach is strong because it absolutely protects individual dignity — no amount of social benefit can justify treating Harrison as a mere means. However, the problem with Deontology is its rigidity. The government might claim it has a duty to maintain social order, and when two duties conflict (duty to individual freedom vs. duty to social stability), Kant offers no clear way to decide which takes priority.

Your Verdict (~100 words)

Having heard both advisors, I find the deontological position more convincing in this case. While my utilitarian advisor correctly identifies the suffering caused by the handicapping system, their framework leaves open a disturbing possibility: that enforced equality could be justified if it made enough people happy. The deontological advisor’s insistence that people must never be treated as mere means provides a firmer foundation for condemning the system. I rule that the handicapping system is unjust because it violates the fundamental dignity of every citizen. In my kingdom, excellence will be celebrated, not punished.

Essential Sources for Your Assignment

All readings are from James Rachels' “The Elements of Moral Philosophy”

Utilitarianism ▼

Rachels traces the utilitarian revolution from Jeremy Bentham to John Stuart Mill. The core idea is simple but radical: the morally right action is the one that produces the greatest happiness for the greatest number (the Principle of Utility). Rachels explores three modern applications:

  • Euthanasia — through the case of Sigmund Freud choosing to end his suffering, Rachels shows how utilitarians weigh quality of life against quantity
  • Marijuana & the War on Drugs — using Mill’s Harm Principle (the only justification for limiting someone’s freedom is to prevent harm to others), Rachels questions whether drug criminalisation actually reduces suffering
  • Nonhuman Animals — Rachels introduces Peter Singer’s concept of speciesism (treating animals as less morally important simply because they’re a different species) and the utilitarian case against factory farming

Key concepts: Principle of Utility, Greatest Happiness Principle, Harm Principle, higher vs lower pleasures, speciesism, impartiality

Rachels - Utilitarianism.pdf
Deontology ▼

Rachels explains Immanuel Kant’s moral philosophy, which holds that morality is about duty and universal rules, not consequences. The key ideas:

  • The Categorical Imperative — act only according to rules you could will to be universal laws. If everyone did what you’re doing, would it still make sense?
  • Treating people as ends — never treat a person merely as a means to your own goals; every rational being has inherent dignity
  • Absolute rules — Rachels presents Kant’s controversial claim that lying is always wrong, even to an “inquiring murderer” at your door asking where your friend is hiding
  • Conflicts between rules — through the Hurricane Katrina mercy killing case, Rachels explores what happens when two moral duties collide

Key concepts: Categorical Imperative, hypothetical vs categorical imperatives, maxim, universal law, treating as ends, absolute moral rules

Rachels - Deontology.pdf
Virtue Ethics ▼

Rachels shifts focus from “What should I do?” to “What kind of person should I be?” This is the question at the heart of Aristotle’s Virtue Ethics:

  • Virtues as character traits — a virtue is not a single good action but a habitual disposition. You become courageous by practising courage, generous by practising generosity
  • The Golden Mean — every virtue lies between two extremes (vices). Courage lies between cowardice (too little) and recklessness (too much). Generosity lies between stinginess and wastefulness
  • Eudaimonia — the ultimate goal of human life is flourishing or “living well.” Virtues are the character traits that enable eudaimonia
  • Real examples — Rachels discusses courage through the 9/11 debates (Bill Maher, Peter Geach), honesty through Athanasius protecting fugitives, and generosity through Jesus’s teachings

Key concepts: virtue, vice, Golden Mean, eudaimonia, character, habitual action, courage, generosity, honesty

Rachels - Virtue Ethics.pdf
Ethical Egoism ▼

Rachels examines the provocative claim that acting in your own self-interest is not only natural but morally right. The key figure is Ayn Rand:

  • Rational self-interest — Rand argues that each person’s primary moral obligation is to pursue their own happiness and well-being. This is not selfishness in the petty sense, but a principled commitment to one’s own rational goals
  • The argument against altruism — Rand claims that altruism (putting others first) is actually self-defeating and degrading. If everyone sacrifices themselves for others, everyone ends up worse off
  • Enlightened egoism — being kind to others can still be rational if it serves your long-term interests (building relationships, earning trust, creating a stable society)
  • Critiques — Rachels examines whether egoism can handle moral dilemmas where self-interest and the common good genuinely conflict

Key concepts: rational self-interest, ethical egoism vs psychological egoism, enlightened egoism, the altruism problem, individual rights
